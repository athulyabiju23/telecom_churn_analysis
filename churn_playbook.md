# Churn Playbook

## whats going on

our churn rate is sitting at 28% which is bad. basically we're losing almost 1 in 3 customers. i analyzed about 10,000 customers from 3 different data sources to figure out why people leave and what we can actually do about it.

## what the data told us

the biggest thing is contract type. month to month customers churn at 48% which is almost half of them walking out. two year contracts? only 2.8%. so the single most important thing is getting people locked into longer contracts.

the first 6 months are brutal. 77% of customers who leave do it in that window. after that the churn rate drops off a cliff. so whatever retention efforts we have need to be front loaded in those early months, not spread evenly across the customer lifecycle.

45% of the people who left said they went to a competitor. not because of price (only 11% said that), not because of billing issues, but because someone else gave them a better offer. this means discounting alone wont fix the problem. we need to actually be competitive on the product side.

customer service calls are a leading indicator. if someone calls support 3 times, their churn rate is still normal around 10%. but the 4th call? it jumps to 46%. by the 5th or 6th call its over 60%. so 4 calls is the red flag.

## what to do about it

**for new customers (first 6 months)**
do a check in call at 30 days and again at 90 days. not a sales call, just asking how things are going. this is when theyre most likely to leave and a small touch point can make a difference. this should be owned by customer success and its high priority.

**for month to month customers under 12 months tenure**
offer them 15% off if they switch to an annual contract. the math works out because even with the discount, keeping them for a year is worth way more than losing them in month 3. sales or retention team owns this.

**when someone calls support 3+ times in a month**
auto escalate to a retention specialist. dont wait for them to say theyre leaving. pull up their full ticket history so the specialist has context. customer success owns this and its high priority.

**when the model flags someone as high risk (>60% churn probability)**
immediate outreach from the retention team. dont send an email, call them. these are the customers who are basically already out the door. this is critical priority.

**high value customers ($80+/mo) on month to month**
assign them a dedicated account manager. these people are paying a lot and theyre the most attractive targets for competitors. account management owns this.

**customers with internet but no tech support**
offer them 3 months of free tech support. the model shows tech support is one of the top churn drivers. getting them to try it might make them stickier. product or sales team.

**customers paying with electronic check**
offer $5/mo discount to switch to auto pay. small thing but e check users have slightly higher churn. billing team can handle this, low priority.

## how much money are we talking about

the high risk group alone is 1,149 customers paying an average of $74/month. thats about $85K in monthly revenue that is likely to walk out the door.

if we can reduce overall churn by just 5%, thats roughly 330 customers saved per year which is about $293K in annual revenue. a 15% reduction gets us to ~990 saved customers and almost $880K per year.

the cost of most retention offers (a discount, a free add on, a phone call) is tiny compared to the cost of acquiring a replacement customer.

## risk tiers

i segmented all customers into 3 tiers based on the model:

**low risk (churn prob under 30%)** - 4,215 customers. average tenure is 43 months, they pay about $60/mo. only 2.6% of them actually churned. no action needed, just keep doing what were doing.

**medium risk (30-60%)** - 1,224 customers. average tenure 29 months, $81/mo. 54% of them churned. these need proactive retention offers before they start looking at competitors.

**high risk (over 60%)** - 1,149 customers. average tenure just 8 months, $74/mo. 95% of them churned. these are almost all new customers who are paying decent money but leaving fast. this is where the biggest opportunity is.

## model predictions vs what customers actually said

this is the most interesting part. the model says tenure and contract type are the biggest predictors of churn. but when you look at what customers actually said when they left, 45% went to a competitor and 17% were unhappy with the service. only 11% mentioned price.

both things are true at the same time. the short tenure month to month customers are the ones most vulnerable to being poached by competitors. so the retention strategy shouldnt just be about discounts. it should be about making the product and service good enough that competitors cant steal your customers in the first place, AND locking people into contracts early so they dont have the option to leave on a whim.
